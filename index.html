Substance use, sexual networks, and HIV

  • Well established link between substance use and HIV risk among sexual minority men
    • In one national cohort, 1 in 3 recent seroconversions among MSM linked to methamphetamine (Grov et al., 2020)
  • Substance use impacts HIV through sexual arousal, risk taking, and schedule disruptions
  • Substance use associated with many HIV related behaviors:
    • Condom use (Vosburgh et al., 2012)
    • Prevention behavior (e.g., pre-exposure prophylaxis [PrEP]; Okafor et al., 2020)
    • Treatment behavior (e.g., anti-retroviral therapy [ART]; Tsuyuki et al., 2019)
    • Network structure (e.g., partnership rate, length of partnerships, homophily, etc.)
  • To shape public health response we need to understand how these behaviors intersect to impact HIV incidence (Torres & Adam, 2016)
    • These behaviors are complex and interdependent
    • Challenging to independently estimate their association with HIV seroconversion
    • How do we identify the relative importance of these mechanisms to improve prevention?
  • Using network based epidemic models to achieve a holistic understanding of substance use and HIV

Modeling substance use and HIV (PUG; R01 DA055502; PI)

  1. Synthesize data on substance use and HIV among MSM
  2. Integrate these parameters into existing epidemic models of HIV
  3. Identify the most important mechanisms of substance use impacting HIV incidence
  4. Experiment to find optimal and robust interventions

Modeling substance use and HIV (PUG; R01 DA055502; PI)

  • Synthesize data on substance use and HIV among MSM (Aim 1)
    • Substance use and sexual networks
      • Differences in partnership rates
      • Substance use homophily (i.e., assortativity)
      • Substance use and partnership duration
    • Substance use epidemiology
      • Prevalence of substances across lifespan
      • Onset and trajectories of use
      • Substance use and prevention/care continuum
  • Integrate these parameters into existing epidemic models of HIV (Aim 2)
    • EpiModel (R01 AI138783; Co-I) to build network based epidemic models
      • Temporal exponential random graph models (ERGMs) to simulate networks
      • Traditional disease transmission over these networks
    • Calibrated to local surveillance data
  • Model exploration using Extreme-scale Model Exploration with Swift (EMEWS)

Substance use and sexual network characteristics

  1. Do MSM who use substances have more partners?
    • Degree is a defining network characteristic
    • Increased sexual activities on days using drugs (Wray et al., 2020)
    • Some evidence of 'multiple sex partners' (Moradi et al., 2022)
    • What type of partners? (e.g., one-time, casual, main)
    • What types of substances?
  2. Are MSM who use substances more likely to have partners who also use substances?
    • Frequently studied for race/ethnicity and age (Grey et al., 2015; Janulis et al., 2018)
    • Can amplify or extend existing disparities (Goodreau et al., 2017)
    • Interact with other group features (e.g., degree differences)
    • Difficult to measure

Partnership rates and substance use

  • Estimate association between substance use and partnership rates (past 6 months)
  • Used young MSM cohort data RADAR (U01 DA036939; Co-I)
    • Up to 5 years of data. Visit every 6 months.
    • Completed comprehensive sexual network interview and survey on substance use
  • Separate models for each outcome (one-time, casual, and main)
  • Used 'hybrid' glmm (negative binomial)
    • Person-mean centered (within) and person-mean (between)
    • \[ Y_{i,t} = \gamma_{00} + \gamma_{01}\overline{X_{i}} + \gamma_{10}(X_{i,t} - \overline{X_{i}}) + u_{0i} + e_{i,t} \]
  • Figure shows within-person associations

Substance use homophily in sexual networks of MSM

  • Use data from RADAR to build participant-to-participant sexual network
  • From egocentric network data, used entity resolution used to match participants (R21 LM012578; PI)
  • Self-reported substance use to categorize individuals
  • ERGMs used to estimate substance use homophily controlling for other features
    • Similar to logistic regression
    • Predicting probability of a tie between two given nodes
    • Can use features of nodes, edges, and network structure
  • Figure shows any non-marijuana drug use
